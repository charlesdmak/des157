THE WAR ON DRUGS

WAS JUST AN EXCUSE TO LOCK US UP

A BRIEF HISTORY ON THE WAR

Drugs became associated with youthful rebellion, and scientific research ended.

President Nixon declares "war on drugs". Increased marijuana charges.

Reagan Era saw skyrocketing rates of incarceration for nonviolent drug offenses.

Beginning of a movement to find a more sensible approach to drug policy.

The War on Drugs looked like a systematic attack

Crack cocaine rose in popularity in areas like New York and Los Angeles. It is identical to powder cocaine. The difference is that charges associated with crack were vastly more severe than those associated with the powder. As a result, the levels of arrests and incarcerations continued to rise and prisons were continuing to fill up.

The Real Impacts

When the war on drugs began in 1971, our prison population was 200,000. Today it is over 2,000,000 - nearly a 1000% increase. Mandatory sentences were inacted to make ensure offenders were locked up, and judges were more likely to hand out sentences for people of color.

Whites ~20%

Latinos ~37%

Blacks ~40%

The War on drugs targeted the poor. There was a disproportionate rate of incarcerations for Blacks and Latinos due to certain groups being associated with crack cocaine and marijuana.

Blacks in the US make up 13% of the population. Latinos make up 17%. That's only about 30% of the entire population. Can you guess how many are in federal prison for drug violations?

Together, Blacks and Latinos make up about 80% of those in federal prison due to drug charges. Whites, on the other hand, are about 60% of the US population - yet those in federal prison only consist of roughly 20%.

The disproportionate prosecution of people of color begs the question of what the war on drugs actually achieved. Yes, it may have punished drug users - but it undoubtedly also solidified a first and second-class population.
